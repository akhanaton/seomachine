## 3. Cambridge 9709 Syllabus Scope — Topic 1.2 Functions

### What the 2026-2027 Syllabus Requires (Pure Mathematics 1)

Students should be able to:

1. **Understand the terms** function, domain, range (image set), one-one function, inverse function, and composition of functions

2. **Identify the range** of a given function in simple cases, and find the composition of two given functions

3. **Determine whether or not** a given function is one-one, and find the inverse of a one-one function in simple cases

4. **Illustrate graphically** the relation between a one-one function and its inverse (including the mirror line y = x)

5. **Understand and use** the transformations of the graph y = f(x) given by:
   - y = f(x) + a (vertical translation)
   - y = f(x + a) (horizontal translation)
   - y = af(x) (vertical stretch, scale factor a)
   - y = f(ax) (horizontal stretch, scale factor 1/a)
   - Simple combinations of these transformations

6. **Use the terms** 'translation', 'reflection' and 'stretch' in describing transformations

7. The condition that a composite function gf can only be formed when the **range of f is within the domain of g**

### What's NOT in Pure 1 (but IS in Pure 3)
- **Modulus function** |f(x)| — Pure 3 only
- **Partial fractions** — Pure 3 only (algebraic, not functions per se)
- **Exponential and logarithmic functions** as a distinct topic — Pure 3 (though basic exponentials may appear)
- **Polynomial functions of degree 3 or 4** — Pure 3
- Knowledge of Pure 1 is assumed in Pure 3 — candidates may need to demonstrate functions skills in Pure 3 questions

### Function Types Tested in Pure 1
- **Quadratic functions** (most common — find inverse with restricted domain, complete the square to find range)
- **Linear functions** (straightforward inverses)
- **Simple rational functions** (e.g., f(x) = (2x+1)/(x-3))
- **Square root functions** (e.g., f(x) = √(2x-1))
- **Trigonometric functions** in graph transformation context
- Functions may be defined piecewise in exam questions

### Common Exam Question Formats for Functions in 9709
1. **"Find the range of f"** — requires completing the square or sketching to identify minimum/maximum
2. **"Find f⁻¹(x) and state its domain"** — algebraic inverse + domain = range of original
3. **"Find fg(x) and state its range"** — form composite, then find range
4. **"Explain why f does not have an inverse"** — horizontal line test / many-one argument, then restrict domain
5. **"Describe a sequence of transformations"** — given before/after graphs or equations
6. **"Show that the function is self-inverse"** — prove ff(x) = x
7. **"Sketch y = f(x) and y = f⁻¹(x) on the same diagram"** — with y = x mirror line
8. **"State the domain of gf"** — requires checking range of f against domain of g
9. **"Solve f(x) = f⁻¹(x)"** — intersection lies on y = x, so solve f(x) = x

## 4. Student Pain Points (From Forums & Examiner Reports)

### What Students Actually Struggle With

1. **Domain and range confusion** — The #1 pain point for functions. Students confuse which is which, struggle to find the range of quadratic functions, and don't understand why the range matters for inverses. The Student Room has multiple threads from students saying "I don't understand how to find the range" and "what's the difference between domain and range."

2. **Composite function order (fg(x) vs gf(x))** — Students consistently apply the functions in the wrong order. The common error is reading fg(x) left-to-right (applying f first, then g) when it should be g first, then f. Students on forums describe this as "confusing" and "counter-intuitive." The notation fg(x) = f(g(x)) needs explicit teaching.

3. **"Why can't I find the inverse?"** — Students don't connect one-one functions to the existence of inverses. When asked "explain why f does not have an inverse," they struggle to articulate that a many-one function (like a quadratic without domain restriction) maps two x-values to the same y-value, so the inverse wouldn't be a function.

4. **Graph transformations in the x-direction** — The single most confusing aspect of transformations. Students understand that y = f(x) + 3 shifts UP by 3, but cannot intuit why y = f(x + 3) shifts LEFT by 3 (not right). Similarly, y = f(2x) is a horizontal stretch with scale factor 1/2 (not 2). The "opposite to what you'd expect" nature of horizontal transformations is a persistent source of errors.

5. **Finding the range by completing the square** — For quadratic functions like f(x) = x² - 4x + 7, students need to complete the square to find the minimum/maximum, then state the range. Many either can't complete the square reliably or don't connect the vertex to the range.

6. **Domain of composite functions** — Students form fg(x) algebraically but don't check whether the range of g is within the domain of f. Examiner reports flag this as a recurring error — students produce an algebraic expression for the composite but ignore domain restrictions.

7. **Inverse function mistakes** — Three common errors: (a) not swapping x and y before rearranging, (b) stating the wrong domain for f⁻¹ (forgetting it's the range of f), (c) confusing f⁻¹(x) with 1/f(x) due to the superscript -1 notation.

8. **Self-inverse function proofs** — Students don't know what "self-inverse" means or how to prove it. The method (show ff(x) = x) is simple but students often overcomplicate it.

9. **Combining transformations** — When a graph requires multiple transformations (e.g., y = 2f(x-1) + 3), students struggle with the order of operations. The standard approach is: deal with the inside of the brackets first (horizontal transformations), then outside (vertical).

10. **Sketching f and f⁻¹ on the same axes** — Students forget the y = x mirror line, or draw the inverse incorrectly. They also forget to label key points on both graphs.

### Examiner Report Insights (Cambridge 9709)

From Cambridge 9709 examiner reports (2019-2024):

1. **Inverse function mechanics** — "Many candidates were more confident with the standard inverse function requirements" but errors occur when inverses involve quadratics (completing the square step trips students up) or when students fail to state the correct domain for f⁻¹.

2. **One-one confusion** — "A significant number of candidates thought that potentially square rooting a negative number was the issue, rather than the function not being one to one" — students confuse domain restrictions (to make a function defined) with domain restrictions (to make a function one-one for an inverse to exist). This is a fundamental conceptual misunderstanding.

3. **Insufficient working** — Examiner reports consistently flag candidates who produce answers without showing the algebraic steps, particularly in "show that" style inverse function questions.

4. **Graph transformation descriptions** — Candidates lose marks by not using correct terminology (e.g., saying "shift" instead of "translation" or omitting the direction/scale factor). The syllabus explicitly requires use of the terms 'translation', 'reflection', and 'stretch'.

5. **Composite function formation** — Errors in the order of application (fg vs gf) and in simplifying the resulting expression. Students who write fg(x) = f(x) × g(x) (treating it as multiplication) appear in examiner reports.

6. **Range identification** — Students often state the range of a quadratic without completing the square or identifying the vertex first. Simply substituting a few x-values is not a valid method.
