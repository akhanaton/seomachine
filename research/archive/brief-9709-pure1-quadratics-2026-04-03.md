## 3. Student Pain Points (From Forums & Examiner Reports)

### What Students Actually Struggle With

1. **Completing the square errors** (Very High) -- Sign errors when completing the square, especially with negative leading coefficients. Students write a(x + p)^2 + q but get p or q wrong. Examiner reports cite this consistently.

2. **Not recognising hidden quadratics** (High) -- Equations like 2^(2x) - 5(2^x) + 4 = 0 or sin^2 x + sin x - 2 = 0 are quadratics in disguise. Students don't see the substitution (let u = 2^x or u = sin x). This appears in trigonometry and indices questions, not just "quadratics" questions.

3. **Discriminant condition confusion** (High) -- Students mix up > 0, = 0, < 0 conditions, especially in applied contexts ("find the range of k for which the equation has no real roots"). The language trips them up.

4. **Quadratic inequalities** (High) -- Students can find critical values but don't know how to determine the solution set. Drawing the parabola and reading the inequality from the sketch is the reliable method, but many try to use algebra alone.

5. **Simultaneous equations (quadratic-linear)** (Medium) -- The substitution is mechanical but students make errors when substituting a linear expression into a quadratic. Expanding brackets incorrectly is the most common issue.

6. **"Show that" / proof-style quadratic questions** (Medium) -- Setting up the quadratic from a word problem or geometric context, then applying the discriminant. The setup is the hard part.

7. **Fractional/negative coefficients** (Medium) -- Quadratics with fractions (like 1/2 x^2 - 3x + 1 = 0) cause algebraic errors. Students prefer to multiply through by 2 but don't always do it correctly.

8. **Not checking answers back into the original equation** (Medium) -- Particularly with hidden quadratics where a substitution was used. Students find u but forget to convert back to x, or accept a negative value for something that must be positive (e.g., 2^x cannot be negative).
