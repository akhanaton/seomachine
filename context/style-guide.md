# ExamPilot Style Guide

## Grammar & Mechanics

### Capitalisation
- **ExamPilot**: Always capitalised as one word with capital E and P
- **Ask Sparky**: Both words capitalised (it's a product name)
- **Exam Readiness Index (ERI)**: Capitalised, abbreviation in parentheses on first use
- **Question DNA**: Both words capitalised
- **Smart Review**: Both words capitalised
- **ExamLens**: One word, capital E and L
- **A-Level**: Always hyphenated, capital A and L
- **GCSE**: Always uppercase
- **Cambridge 9709**: Cambridge capitalised, spec code as numbers
- **Edexcel IAL**: Edexcel capitalised, IAL uppercase
- **FSRS**: Always uppercase (it's an algorithm name)
- **Pure 1, Pure 2, etc.**: Capital P, number not spelled out

### Numbers
- Spell out one through nine in prose
- Use numerals for 10 and above
- Always use numerals for: grades (A*, A, B), percentages (78%), scores, exam paper numbers
- Use numerals in headlines for scannability

### Punctuation
- Oxford comma: Yes, always
- Em dashes: Use sparingly — they work for asides but don't overdo it
- Exclamation marks: Maximum one per article. We're confident, not excited.
- Ellipsis: Avoid in published content

### UK vs US English
- **Use UK English** as the default: colour, analyse, organise, practise (verb), practice (noun)
- Exception: technical terms that are universally US English (e.g., "program" for software)
- Cambridge and Edexcel use UK English in their specifications — match them

## Formatting Standards

### Headlines
- Be specific: "How to Improve Your Cambridge 9709 Pure 1 Score in 4 Weeks" not "Maths Revision Tips"
- Include the exam board when relevant
- Use numbers for listicles
- Keep under 60 characters for SEO
- Front-load the keyword

### Subheadings (H2, H3)
- Descriptive enough to scan — reader should grasp the article from headings alone
- Include keywords naturally
- Use H2 for main sections, H3 for subsections
- Never skip heading levels (no H2 → H4)

### Paragraphs
- 1-3 sentences maximum
- One idea per paragraph
- Mobile-first: think about how this looks on a phone screen
- Use line breaks generously

### Lists
- Numbered for sequential steps
- Bulleted for non-sequential points
- Consistent structure (all start with verbs, or all are noun phrases)
- Keep items concise — if a list item needs a paragraph of explanation, it shouldn't be a list

### Code / Technical Content
- Use code formatting for: file names, URLs, technical terms on first appearance
- Keep technical explanations brief — link to docs for detail

### Images
- Alt text: descriptive, keyword-inclusive
- Captions: brief, adds context not visible in the image
- Mobile-responsive: ensure images don't break mobile layouts

## Content Length Guidelines

| Content Type | Word Count | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Standard blog post | 1,500-2,500 | Target 2,000 |
| Pillar/hub page | 3,000-5,000 | Comprehensive coverage |
| Topic page | 800-1,500 | Focused, exam-board specific |
| Comparison page | 1,500-2,000 | Feature-by-feature analysis |
| How-to guide | 1,000-2,000 | Step-by-step, practical |

## SEO Writing Rules

- Primary keyword in title, first paragraph, at least one H2, and meta description
- Secondary keywords distributed naturally throughout
- Internal links: minimum 2 per article (see internal-links-map.md)
- External links: 1-3 per article to authoritative sources (exam boards, research)
- Meta description: 150-160 characters, includes primary keyword, compelling click reason
- URL slug: lowercase, hyphenated, keyword-rich, under 60 characters

## Calls to Action

### Primary CTAs (for bottom-of-funnel content)
- "Try ExamPilot free" (when available)
- "Join the waitlist" (pre-launch)
- "Check your exam readiness"

### Secondary CTAs (for top/mid-funnel content)
- "Read our Cambridge 9709 revision guide"
- "Explore more study strategies"
- "See how ExamPilot works"

### CTA Placement
- One above the fold (within first 300 words)
- One at the end of the article
- Optional: one mid-article if the post is 2,000+ words
- Never more than 3 CTAs per article

## What We Don't Do

- We don't use clickbait headlines
- We don't make unsubstantiated claims ("the best app ever")
- We don't publish thin content (<500 words) as standalone articles
- We don't keyword-stuff
- We don't use AI-generated content without human review and substantial editing
- We don't publish content that doesn't serve the student first
